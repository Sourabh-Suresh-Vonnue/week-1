The quantum future is still wide open

Anybody who says they understand quantum computing is lying. You can do the maths for quantum mechanics, but nobody has falsifiably proven what it physically means. There's a gap between predicting outcomes (which we can do) and understanding what causes them (which we don't). That's why we have not one explanation, but a handful of competing interpretations. In quantum physics, particles don't seem to 'choose' a position or state until we check. So the core puzzle is: where is a particle before we measure it? You've probably heard of Schrödinger's cat, the idea that it's both dead and alive until we look.

The two most common interpretations, Copenhagen and Many Worlds, offer very different answers even though neither has been proven yet. The Copenhagen interpretation (Team Bohr!) says the system really is in multiple states at once (called a superposition) until someone measures it. Measurement causes 'collapse', forcing the system into a single outcome. But this still leaves us wondering: what counts as a measurement? A person? A camera? We don't know. The Many Worlds interpretation is classic sci-fi. The superposition is the same but the system never collapses. Instead, reality splits into multiple universes. One version sees a dead cat, another sees it alive, and both continue in parallel. Measurement doesn't destroy possibilities, it just reveals which universe you're in. Both interpretations explain the same results, that particles lack definite positions until measured, but they tell very different stories. So far, we can't tell which (if either) is true.

When You Feel Lost, There Is One Way Forward

Most of us have been there — the moment when the plan falls apart, the relationship fractures, the diagnosis comes in, or life just stops making sense. You're not physically lost, but something inside you feels completely unmoored. You don't know what to do next. You don't know who to trust. You barely know what to believe anymore. And in those moments, there's no shortage of voices telling you which way to go. In John 13-14, Jesus is sitting with His disciples in the hours before His arrest — and they are unraveling. He's told them He's leaving. He's told them they can't follow. Everything they thought they understood about what He came to do is suddenly in question. And into that confusion, Jesus speaks one of the most direct and remarkable statements in all of Scripture: "I am the way, the truth, and the life. No one comes to the Father except through me" (John 14:6, CSB). This is Part 5 of our I AM series at Newbreak — a San Diego church with campuses in Tierrasanta, Scripps Ranch, and Ocean Beach — and this week, we're asking what that statement actually means for us today.

Weeding the Forest

In a healthy garden, weeding is a grueling but necessary task if we want our desired plants to thrive. A garden that has gone too long without weeding can become “choked out.” Water ends up being wasted in an unweeded garden, as the weeds absorb most of it, continuing to flourish while choking out our favored plants. Similarly, forests can also become choked out or overcrowded.

At Pepperwood, we sometimes employ a practice called forest thinning if a forest is crowded. To improve the health and promote old growth trees in an overgrown forest, we selectively remove smaller unhealthy trees – essentially, weeding the forest. Much like our gardens, thinning an overly crowded forest allows the remaining trees to access more water, light, and nutrients, making them less vulnerable to pests and enjoying better overall health. This is a boon to the ecosystem at large, not just for the trees themselves.
